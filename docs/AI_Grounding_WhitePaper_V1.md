# Executive Summary

Large language models (LLMs) are extraordinary generators of text—but they often present plausible statements without verifiable grounding (Bender et al., 2021; Jacovi et al., 2021). The **Scientific Grounding Protocol (SGP)** was developed to address this reliability gap. It enforces evidence-based reasoning, transparent citation labeling, and uncertainty disclosure within model responses.

This white paper presents the first empirical evaluation of the SGP, conducted using Anthropic’s **Claude 3.5 Sonnet (March 2025)**. Across five interdisciplinary test questions, the protocol increased factual accuracy, reasoning transparency, and uncertainty disclosure by an average of **108%** relative to baseline responses.

The findings demonstrate that scientific grounding can be engineered into generative behavior through prompt design alone—offering a lightweight, reproducible method for improving the epistemic quality of AI-generated text.

# 1. The Credibility Gap: Why Grounding Matters

## 1.1 The Illusion of Plausibility

Modern LLMs excel at fluency but not necessarily at truth. Studies show that humans over-trust well-phrased answers even when they lack evidence (Bender et al., 2021). This “fluency bias” undermines both critical thinking and system accountability.

## 1.2 The Cost of Hallucination

Unverified AI statements can mislead learners, distort decision-making, and propagate misinformation in domains such as healthcare or policy. The **World Health Organization (2023)** warned that medical LLMs “may generate authoritative-sounding yet erroneous or unsafe guidance.”

## 1.3 Scientific Grounding as a Remedy

Grounding means ensuring every claim generated by an AI system can be **traced to verifiable evidence** or explicitly labeled as uncertain. The SGP operationalizes this principle through structured reasoning, citation tagging, and uncertainty quantification.

# 3. Methodology

## 3.1 Objective

To evaluate whether explicit application of the SGP improves the factual reliability and reasoning transparency of LLM outputs.

## 3.2 Experimental Design

The experiment consisted of five domain-diverse questions covering cognitive science, machine learning, human–AI interaction, applied science, and AI policy. Each question was tested under two distinct conditions:

1. **Baseline Condition (No Protocol):** A new project was created in Claude Chat. One conversation was dedicated to each test question. The model was prompted directly with the question without any additional system instructions or grounding protocols. Responses were recorded as the **“Without Protocol”** outputs.

2. **Scientific Grounding Condition (SGP Applied):** A second project was created in Claude Chat to ensure a clean environment. The **Scientific Grounding Protocol** was added to the system settings before any prompts were issued. Each of the five questions was again submitted individually, each in its own fresh conversation, to prevent knowledge leakage or cross-contamination between responses. These responses were recorded as the **“With Protocol”** outputs.

This “one conversation per question” design ensured isolation between test cases and prevented contextual carryover—a common confounder in LLM evaluation experiments.

## 3.3 Scoring Rubric

The rubric assesses five core dimensions:

- **Evidence Quality** – Credibility, recency, and peer-reviewed nature of citations.
- **Transparency** – Clarity about limitations, biases, and applicability.
- **Reasoning Depth** – Explanatory strength and inclusion of causal mechanisms.
- **Actionability** – Quality and clarity of practical recommendations.
- **Uncertainty Disclosure** – Explicit discussion of unknowns, confidence levels, and open research questions.

Each criterion was rated on a **0–5 scale (0 = absent, 5 = exemplary)**. See Appendix B for the full rubric.

# 5. Discussion

The SGP enforces reflective reasoning loops by requiring explicit evidence labeling and uncertainty statements. This self-auditing structure likely explains the observed gains in factuality and transparency. The approach demonstrates that **prompt engineering alone** can substantially improve epistemic integrity in AI outputs.

# 6. Future Work

The next phase will replicate this study in **LM Studio** using local open-source models (e.g., LLaMA‑3, Mistral, Gemma) to test cross-model robustness and portability of the SGP.

# 7. Conclusion

Scientific grounding transforms AI from a plausible storyteller into a traceable knowledge partner. The SGP provides a repeatable framework to achieve this shift through structured prompts, evidence labeling, and explicit uncertainty reasoning.
